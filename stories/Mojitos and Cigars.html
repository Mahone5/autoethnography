Mojitos and Cigars

"Peace, that glorious moment in time when everyone stops and reloads." --Thomas Jefferson

The sixth-grade girls in the neighborhood lead the pack for the morning walk to school. I carry my lunch and my baseball glove, no books or homework. My father, basically self-taught and strongly against homework, says, "They have you for six-and-a-half hours a day, what in the world are they doing all day?"

One thing our school prepares us well for is nuclear attack. We could have a nuke dropped on us at any moment. We have regular drills marching down to the basement, or getting under our desk. This reminds me of when I'm at work in Japan and we get under the desk for an earthquake, like that's going to help when the whole building collapses. Sonic booms are common. They are loud, and knock stuff off a shelf or sometimes even break windows. According to Thomas Jefferson Elementary School, Moscow is a similar size and shape to St. Louis, so it's a good place to practice dropping nuclear bombs. I write a poem about about the situation. "Ninety miles from our shores, lies the nearest of our nation's foes." The island of Mojitos and cigars.

They teach us the three R's. After school, we play baseball, football, get into fights, loiter by the house of the cutest girl in our class, and stop by this one old lady's house who gives us candy. I'll never forget the sound of her doorbell chimes. Sometimes I hear the same chime in Japan. Then back home for dinner, and likely to chapel.

We go to chapel a lot, more than any of my friends at school. Our family has deep, important, and dogmatic theological beliefs held by almost no one else in the world. We distinguish ourselves by calling our meeting place a chapel, definitely not a church. Our path is straight and narrow. Almost all people outside our little flock are bound for eternal damnation, where there is weeping, wailing and gnashing of teeth, forever and ever. Never ending torture. Not many attend our church in order to hear this Good News. For practical living, the mantra is something like "Don't drink, don't chew, and don't go out with the girls that do." In our church, nuclear warfare isn't nearly as bad as Mojitos and cigars.
